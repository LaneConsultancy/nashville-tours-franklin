## About Diana Berry

### H2: Why I Started Nashville Tours Franklin

I've been showing people around Franklin for years — friends who came to visit, relatives passing through Nashville, colleagues from out of state who'd heard the town was worth a detour. At some point I realized I was doing it anyway, so I might as well do it properly.

Franklin is the kind of place that rewards attention. The more you look, the more you find. A building that looks like a pleasant Victorian storefront turns out to have served as a Confederate field hospital. A quiet residential street runs alongside the ground where two armies tore each other apart on a November night in 1864. The town's founder is buried under one of the most picturesque public squares in the South. None of this is secret — it's all documented — but most visitors walk right past it because nobody's pointed it out yet.

That's the gap I wanted to fill.

Nashville Tours Franklin started as a simple conviction: Franklin deserves guides who treat it seriously. Not as a charming backdrop for a photo opportunity, but as a place with real history, real people, and stories that actually matter. I built every tour around that belief.

---

### H2: My Connection to Franklin

I've lived in and around Williamson County long enough to remember Franklin before it made the national lists of best small towns. I've watched the food scene develop from a handful of places to something genuinely remarkable. I've talked to long-time residents who remember the town before the restoration of Main Street, and to historians at the Carter House and Carnton who've spent decades piecing together what happened here in November 1864.

That accumulation of conversations, research, and time is what I bring to every tour.

Franklin's history isn't just the Civil War, though that alone could fill a dozen tours. It's also the founding families who built this town on the frontier in 1799. The antebellum architecture that survived because the community decided it mattered. The music industry that quietly relocated here over the past three decades, filling the old estates and new builds with some of the biggest names in country music. The food scene that grew up around all of it.

I love this town. That's probably the most important thing to know about me as a guide — the enthusiasm is not a performance.

### H2: What Makes a Nashville Tours Franklin Experience Different

Most tour operators give you facts with a narrative arc. I try to give you something closer to a conversation — one where you feel like you've genuinely met the place.

That means keeping groups small enough that questions are welcome. It means not rushing past the things that deserve more time. It means knowing when a story needs the full version and when a single detail is enough to make a building come alive.

It also means doing the research. I've spent time in archives, in the collections at the Carter House and Carnton, in conversations with local historians and longtime residents. The stories I tell are grounded in primary sources and real accounts, not tour-guide mythology passed down from previous operators. When I'm not sure about something, I say so. When I know something that isn't commonly known, I tell you.

Nashville Tours Franklin offers five distinct tour types — ghost and haunted history, historic walking, food and spirits, celebrity homes and filming locations, and private custom experiences. Each one is built around a specific kind of curiosity. Most guests start with one and come back for another.
