## Opening

On the evening of November 30, 1864, more than 9,000 men fell at the Battle of Franklin in five hours of close-quarters combat. The fighting was so savage, so compressed, and so complete that the town couldn't recover its dead for days. Soldiers were buried where they fell. Houses became hospitals, then morgues. The Carter House — still standing at the center of Franklin — sat directly in the path of the Confederate charge, and the bullet holes in its walls have never been repaired, because nobody could bring themselves to.

Franklin, Tennessee carries that night with it. It always has.

The Nashville Tours Franklin ghost and haunted history tour doesn't rely on theatrical scares or costumed actors jumping out of doorways. What we offer is something that holds up better than theater: the actual history. When you understand what happened in these buildings, on these streets, in this exact ground under your feet — that's when Franklin gets genuinely unsettling in the best possible way.

## What You'll Experience

### H2: The Haunted Heart of Franklin

The tour covers the most historically significant and paranormally documented sites in Franklin's downtown historic district. Diana Berry has spent years researching the history and the documented encounters at each location, drawing on firsthand accounts, archive research, and conversations with staff at the historic properties who've experienced things they can't fully explain.

**H3: The Carter House**
The Carter family sheltered in their basement while Confederate and Union forces fought the bloodiest close-range combat of the war directly outside — and in some cases through — their home. The facade is still pocked with original bullet holes. Multiple accounts from staff, guides, and overnight guests over the years describe sounds, movements, and presences that don't have easy explanations. The Carter House is the most documented haunted site in Franklin, and the stories associated with it are among the most substantiated you'll find anywhere in the South.

**H3: Carnton Plantation**
Three miles from Carter House, Carnton served as the Confederate field hospital for the battle's aftermath. Four Confederate generals were laid out on the back porch. The house absorbed so much loss in those days after the battle — soldiers died in nearly every room — that the McGavock family eventually established the largest private Confederate cemetery in the country on their grounds. The house itself has been the subject of documented paranormal accounts for decades, reported by staff and visitors who weren't looking for anything unusual.

**H3: The Downtown Historic District**
Several buildings along Franklin's Main Street carry their own histories — structures that served military functions during and after the battle, private homes where the aftermath played out for weeks, and establishments with stories that have passed through generations of Franklin families. Diana covers the buildings you'd walk past without a second glance and gives them back their weight.

**H3: Cherry Manor**
Cherry Manor appears in accounts of Franklin's haunted reputation more consistently than almost anywhere else. The history of the property, its role in the community, and the nature of the encounters reported there give the tour one of its most lingering stories — one that visitors tend to bring up when describing the tour afterward.

## Practical Information

### H2: What to Expect and What to Bring

**Comfortable shoes.** The tour covers about three-quarters of a mile on sidewalks and paved paths. Franklin's terrain is flat and accessible.

**A layer in cooler months.** Evening tours in fall and winter can be brisk. October — Franklin's busiest ghost tour month — is beautiful but cool after dark.

**Your phone or camera.** Photos are encouraged throughout the tour. Some guests report unusual results; most report excellent ones.

**An open mind rather than high expectations.** This is not a haunted house attraction with jump scares. It's a history tour that takes the paranormal seriously. Guests who come for the history almost always leave satisfied; guests who come exclusively for supernatural encounters should know what the format is.

**No food or drinks required.** The tour is 90 minutes and the pace is leisurely. There's no physical challenge involved.

## FAQ

### H2: Ghost Tour Questions Answered

**H3: Is the ghost tour scary?**
It's genuinely atmospheric and historically intense — not a jump-scare attraction. Most guests describe it as unsettling in the best possible way, particularly when the Civil War history sinks in. It's appropriate for people who enjoy a well-told ghost story; it is not a horror experience.

**H3: Is it suitable for children?**
We recommend ages 12 and up. The content is historically mature — the Battle of Franklin involved mass casualties, and the tour doesn't sanitize that. Younger children who are sensitive to dark historical content may find it overwhelming. Teenagers who are interested in history typically love it.

**H3: Do you use actors or jump scares?**
No. Nashville Tours Franklin ghost tours are storytelling experiences, not theatrical productions. Everything Diana tells you is drawn from documented historical accounts and recorded paranormal reports. The tour format is conversational, not performative.

**H3: What is the most haunted building in Franklin, Tennessee?**
The Carter House is the most extensively documented haunted site in Franklin. The building stood at the epicenter of the battle, has original bullet holes still visible in the walls, and has generated paranormal accounts from multiple independent sources over decades. Carnton Plantation is a close second, with particularly well-documented accounts from the period immediately following the battle.

**H3: What if it rains?**
Tours run in light rain. We cancel only for lightning, tornado warnings, or temperatures that are genuinely unsafe. If we cancel on our end, you receive a full refund or rebooking at no charge. Honestly, some guests say the rain makes the whole thing more atmospheric.

**H3: Can I take photographs during the tour?**
Absolutely. Photography is encouraged. Diana will point out locations that have produced interesting photographic results for previous guests.
